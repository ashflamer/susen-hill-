The Scars Within: Analysis of "On the Face of It"

This interactive report explores Susan Hill’s dramatic work, "On the Face of It," as a profound exploration of isolation, acceptance, and the debilitating psychological consequences of physical abnormality. This application deconstructs the key themes and character arcs to provide a clear, consumable analysis of the play's core message.

Statement of Purpose

This project endeavors to conduct an examination of the theme of **inferiority complex**, specifically analyzing how physical disfigurement and resultant social ostracism fundamentally influence the personality development of the protagonist, Derry. Furthermore, the analysis scrutinizes the therapeutic efficacy of Mr. Lamb's optimistic philosophy in mitigating this self-limiting belief system, thereby establishing the play's function as a critique of pervasive societal prejudice.

Key Thematic Concepts

Isolation vs. Connection

Exploring the self-imposed isolation of Derry versus the radical inclusivity of Mr. Lamb.

Prejudice & Stigma

Analyzing the societal "gaze" and its power to inflict psychological trauma.

Perception & Reality

Deconstructing the "face of it" to find the true, internal self.

Author & Play Context

Understanding the author and the play's background provides a crucial foundation for analyzing its themes. The work is not just a story, but a piece of social commentary delivered through a concise, powerful dramatic format.

About the Author: Susan Hill

Susan Hill is an English author of fiction and non-fiction works. Her novels include *The Woman in Black*, *The Mist in the Mirror*, and *I'm the King of the Castle*, for which she received the Somerset Maugham Award. Her writing is often characterized by its atmospheric tension, psychological depth, and exploration of moral ambiguity. *On the Face of It* showcases her ability to delve into complex human emotions within a minimalist structure.

About the Play: "On the Face of It"

*On the Face of It* is a one-act play that revolves around two characters: Derry, a young boy with a face disfigured by acid, and Mr. Lamb, an old man with a prosthetic tin leg. The entire play is a single, extended conversation in Mr. Lamb's garden. It powerfully contrasts the cynicism and despair of youth, born from societal cruelty, with the optimism and acceptance of age, born from experience. It serves as a potent critique of prejudice and a testament to the healing power of genuine human connection.

The Complex: Deconstructing Derry

Derry's character is a case study in the formation and manifestation of an inferiority complex. His conflict is not with his physical scar, but with the psychological identity he has built around it. This section breaks down the "why" and "how" of his complex.

The Genesis: External Cruelty

The complex is instigated not by the acid, but by the consistent, detrimental responses from society. Public scrutiny, whispers, and pity function as perpetual trauma.

  • He is convinced his appearance renders him "ugly as a devil."
  • He recalls someone saying, "He’d have been better off dead."
  • This internalized self-loathing is externalized as a defense mechanism.

Conceptual Sources of the Complex

Manifestation: Defensive Hostility

Derry's complex is apparent in his defensive and hostile demeanor. He expects revulsion and preemptively attacks to minimize emotional distress.

  • **Cynicism:** Assumes negative judgment from everyone.
  • **Isolation:** Retreats from the world to avoid exposure.
  • **Resentment:** Expresses anger at the world and his perceived fate.

The Catalyst: Mr. Lamb's Philosophy

Mr. Lamb, also marked by disability, functions as the vital therapeutic counterpoint to Derry's despair. He systematically dismantles Derry's self-imposed restrictions by recontextualizing the very idea of "difference."

Derry's Mindset (The "Weed")

  • Focuses on deficiency.
  • Sees difference as ugly.
  • Believes he is unwanted, like a weed.
  • Builds walls to keep people out.
  • Sees the world as a threat.

Mr. Lamb's Philosophy (The "Flower")

  • Focuses on potential.
  • Sees difference as interesting.
  • Believes all things have value ("...a flower, if you like").
  • Keeps his gate open.
  • Sees the world as full of life.

The Core Intervention: Rejection of Labels

Mr. Lamb’s primary tool is the rejection of labels. He draws parallels between Derry’s appearance and elements of nature (bees, crabs, weeds) to avoid societal ideals of perfection. His guiding principle is the primacy of perspective. By asking, "You could get all over, couldn’t you?", he forces Derry to admit his confinement is mental, not physical. He teaches that while society imposes labels, the individual holds the sole authority to reject them.

The Transformation: Derry's Journey

Derry's transformation is an active process, not a sudden event. It culminates in his decision to defy his mother and return to the garden—a definitive act of self-affirmation. Click each step to see the analysis.

1. Encounter & Hostility

Derry enters the garden expecting rejection. He is defensive, hostile, and cynical, projecting his self-hatred onto Mr. Lamb.

"I thought this was an empty place. I didn't know there was anybody here."

2. Conflict & Curiosity

Mr. Lamb's refusal to be shocked or to pity Derry confuses him. His philosophical questions ("What are you afraid of?") break through Derry's defenses, sparking curiosity.

"You don't get upset... You ask questions I don't understand."

3. Revelation & Shift

Derry begins to see his confinement as mental, not physical. He realizes he has the power to choose his perspective, just as Mr. Lamb chooses to see "weeds" as "flowers."

"Things just are. They... don't have to mean anything."

4. Defiance & Decision

At home, Derry defies his mother's attempts to isolate him. This is the first time he asserts his own will against the identity imposed on him.

"I want to... I want to go there. And sit and... listen to things. Listen and look."

5. Self-Realization & Action

Derry's return to the garden is his final act of self-affirmation. He has broken free of his complex, choosing connection over fear, even if he arrives too late to tell Mr. Lamb.

"If I don't go back there, I'll never go anywhere in this world again."

Real-World Application

The play's themes are urgently relevant today. The "Societal Gaze" that traumatized Derry finds a powerful modern equivalent in the "Digital Gaze" of social media, which breeds comparison and inferiority complexes on a global scale.

The Digital Gaze vs. Societal Gaze

Social media platforms emphasize superficial perfection, creating relentless, unattainable standards. This constant exposure to manufactured ideals can lead to anxiety, withdrawal, and inadequacy, directly paralleling Derry’s isolation.

The play teaches that the path to healing is internal self-acceptance, urging us to apply Mr. Lamb’s philosophy: **focus on potential, not deficiency, and reject harmful labels.**
